# Talking to a Republican Elder

A fictional conversation between a young man and a Republican Elder. Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The young man had waited three weeks for this appointment. He'd ironed his only good shirt the night before, but by the time he reached the office his hands were shaking and the shirt was damp at the collar.

The Republican elder sat behind a desk that had probably been there since Eisenhower. He was a large, slow man with white hair and the particular stillness of someone who had survived many angry visitors.

The young man didn't bother with pleasantries.

"Where the fuck is my salary and house at? What have you been doing with the treasury and the sting operations?"

The elder didn't flinch. He'd heard worse before lunch.

"That's no way to talk to an elder," he said quietly. "I know these are trying times. Have a seat. Help yourself to these bread crumbs. Hail Mary and praise Jesus with me, before we continue our talk."

The young man bobbed on his feet, jaw tight. He wanted to stay standing. Staying standing felt like keeping something. But his legs were tired and the chair looked solid, and eventually he sat. He bowed his head for the prayer without quite meaning to, the words coming back to him from somewhere childhood had left them.

The bread crumbs were in a small ceramic bowl. He took a handful skeptically, then another.

"These are delicious," he admitted, hating himself slightly. "I've been having to eat premature raw grains for months now. But this doesn't change some of these facts I'm about to spell out to you."

"I figured not," the elder said. "I'm glad you enjoyed them. You can't find bread crumbs as good as those nowadays, since Big Government done fouled things up with the virus that killed more than half of all the crops on big plantations."

The young man leaned forward. "Society has been selfish and insolent. My family members have been pestering me, having unrealistic expectations of me. Your small government put people like me out of work, and under the financial patronage of my family. I should be living somewhere else by now."

The elder raised one large hand. "Hold right there. You know in a lot of countries, the only house you'd ever own is the one your parents pass down to you."

"Then explain how a lot of the immigrants were given houses." The young man's voice cracked slightly. "I'm not on a list of anyone's priorities. I've been made into an invisible person. I've had to put up with everything — the noise, the politics, all of it."

The elder was quiet for a moment. "So it's a world of rations. Maybe some of the immigrants got handed more than they deserved. Maybe some folks really are rotten, greedy megalomaniacs. But there's no guarantee their lives are really that great."

"You're entertaining possibilities," the young man said. "You're not thinking straight. Your small government let bastard real estate developers squeeze me out."

The elder tilted his head. "Are you sure it's small government? Some of these complexes were funded by the federal treasury, practically built on the backs of people who never saw their fair share. Are you sure that's what you're really complaining about?"

The young man opened his mouth, then closed it.

"People like you say you believe in freedom," he finally said. "Then you sent people to cause problems."

The elder sighed — not dismissively, but like a man setting down something heavy.

"I don't know what world you're living in entirely. But if you were a sucker for neoliberal promises, you'd be poor and outcasted by now. And you are. Not because you failed. Because they were operating on old money the whole time. They told you there's a meritocracy, that hard work and good character get you somewhere. Meanwhile they were using their surplus to fund wars and consolidate power. They gambled your future away. They're not looking out for you. Their social security rests on your financial distress."

The young man sat back. Something shifted in his face — not relief, but the particular exhaustion of having a suspicion confirmed.

After a long moment he asked, "Then what should I do to become whole and independent? I'm not marrying anyone until I have my own roof. I don't talk to the people in town anymore. They saw me fall on hard times and they piled on. I wouldn't shed a tear for them. Community means nothing to me right now, not at this level. I can't work, can't make money, and I don't care to share opinions with people who wrote me off."

The elder looked at him for a long time.

"I have to admit, you're in a predicament," he said finally. "I can't solve your problems. Time will — or it won't, and you'll find another way through. There's got to be an opening in the wall somewhere ahead. And believe me, it wasn't Republican elders who built that wall. We couldn't afford to. The people who built it used status and money as bricks. So until you find your way out, you and I are subject to the same broken system. Sorry, friend. That's the honest truth of it."

The young man stood. He didn't shake the elder's hand, but he didn't slam the door either. Then he pulled out his phone.

"I wanted to show you this AI video I created of me killing an elephant with a submachine gun. The AI did the math, and told me it would take two clips to its torso to kill it."

The Republican elder took the phone and watched. He kept his face still, but something behind his eyes went flat. He handed the phone back.

"Just get out," he said. "You better stop with your violence."
